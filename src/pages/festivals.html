A Year of Celebrations

Every season brings a reason to celebrate in Laxmapuram

Festivals are the soul of Laxmapuram. They bring together families, strengthen community bonds, honour the divine, and celebrate the rhythms of nature and agriculture. From the vibrant colours of Bathukamma to the drum-beats of Bonalu, every celebration is a living expression of Telangana's rich cultural heritage.

❄️ Winter Celebrations

January – February

🪁
January 14–16

Makar Sankranti (సంక్రాంతి)

Sankranti is the grandest harvest festival of Telangana and marks the sun's northward journey (Uttarayana). In Laxmapuram, the three-day celebration begins with Bhogi — a bonfire on the eve of Sankranti where families burn old items to welcome new beginnings.

On Sankranti day, freshly harvested rice is cooked as Pongal (sweet) and offered to the Sun God. The skies fill with colourful kites as children and adults compete in kite-flying contests. Haridasus (wandering minstrels) visit homes singing devotional songs in praise of Lord Vishnu, receiving alms of rice, grains, and money. Women draw elaborate rangoli (muggu) patterns at their doorsteps. Traditional sweets — Ariselu, Poli, and Jaggery-based confections — are prepared and shared with neighbours. Cattle are decorated with paint and garlands as part of the Mattu Pongal ceremony honouring the animals that till the land.

🔱
February / March

Maha Shivaratri (శివరాత్రి)

The great night of Lord Shiva is observed with fasting, night-long vigil (jaagaran), and prayers at the Shiva Temple in Laxmapuram. Devotees offer bilva leaves, milk, and sacred water to the Shivalingam.

The Shiva Temple comes alive with oil lamps and incense smoke. Devotees chant "Om Namah Shivaya" through the night. Many villagers observe a strict fast, breaking it only after the morning pooja. The mythological significance — the cosmic wedding of Shiva and Parvati, and the night Shiva performed the Tandava dance — is retold and celebrated with special cultural programmes.

🌸 Spring Festivals

March – April — New beginnings and renewal

🌸
March / April

Ugadi (ఉగాది)

Ugadi marks the Telugu New Year and the beginning of a new Samvatsara (Hindu calendar year). In Laxmapuram, the day begins with an oil bath and new clothes, followed by prayers at the Laxminarasimha Swamy temple and Rama temple.

The centrepiece of Ugadi is Ugadi Pachadi — a unique dish made from six ingredients representing the six tastes and emotions of life: raw mango (surprise/tang), jaggery (happiness/sweetness), neem flowers (sorrow/bitterness), tamarind (disgust/sourness), chili (anger/spice), and salt (fear/saltiness). This symbolic blend is a philosophical reminder that life is a mixture of all emotions.

Families visit elders for blessings, and the Panchanga Sravanam (almanac reading) is performed by priests who read out predictions for the coming year. Special food preparations like Pulihora (tamarind rice), Bobbatlu (stuffed flatbread), and Payasam are shared among neighbours and the less fortunate.

🏹
March / April

Sri Ram Navami (రామ నవమి)

Ram Navami, the birthday of Lord Rama, is celebrated with great devotion at the Sri Rama Temple in Laxmapuram. The festival falls on the ninth day of Chaitra (spring) month.

Devotees observe fasting and gather at the temple for special abhishekam (sacred bath) of the deity with milk, curd, honey, and water. The Ramayana is recited or performed as a skit by local artists. Sita Kalyana — the symbolic wedding of Sita and Rama — is re-enacted as a joyful ceremony. Prasadam of panakam (jaggery water with pepper) and vadapappu (soaked green gram) is distributed to all devotees.

🌧️ Monsoon Festivities

July – August — The Telangana spirit shines

🥁
July / August

Bonalu (బోనాలు)

Bonalu is one of the most important festivals of Telangana, dedicated to the mother goddess Mahakali (and locally to Pochamma / Yellamma Devi). The word "Bonalu" comes from "Bhojanam" meaning food — the offering of cooked rice in pots to the goddess.

Women dressed in colourful new sarees carry pots of cooked rice (bonalu) on their heads to the Pochamma Devi temple, accompanied by the beats of dhol-tasha drums. The procession is a spectacular display of devotion and cultural pride. Rangam (prophecy) is performed by the temple priest or designated women who, in a trance-like state, foretell events for the community.

Bonalu celebrations in Laxmapuram typically span two days, with the entire village participating in the joyful processions. The festival is a symbol of Telangana identity and was officially declared a state festival after the formation of Telangana in 2014.

🎪
Annual (Varies)

Village Jatara (జాతర)

The annual Village Jatara is the biggest community event of the year — a vibrant fair held at the Pochamma / Yellamma Devi temple. The Jatara typically lasts 2–3 days and draws visitors from surrounding villages.

The Jatara features special pooja rituals, animal offerings (traditionally), folk performances, and a colourful fair with stalls selling toys, sweets, bangles, and household items. Folk entertainers, acrobats, and traditional performers add to the festive atmosphere. The Jatara is a time when distant family members return to the village, and long-standing social bonds are renewed. The entire event is managed by the village community and temple committee.

🍂 Autumn Splendour

September – November — Telangana's flower season

🌼
Sept / Oct (Navaratri season)

Bathukamma (బతుకమ్మ)

Bathukamma is the iconic floral festival of Telangana — a nine-day celebration unique to the region. The name means "Mother, come alive" — a prayer to Goddess Gauri (Parvati) to bless the earth and its women with long lives.

Women and girls of Laxmapuram gather seasonal wildflowers — Tangidi (Cassia), Gunugu (Celosia), Bantipaavala, Teku, and others — and stack them in a conical, layered tower resembling a pyramid. Each evening during the nine days, women in new clothes carry their Bathukammas to the village tank or open ground, sing traditional Bathukamma songs in a circle around the floral arrangement, and immerse it in water as an offering to the goddess.

The final day — Saddula Bathukamma — is the grandest, with the largest Bathukammas prepared and a final procession to the water body. The festival is a celebration of womanhood, nature, and the bond between sisters. The Telangana Government declared Bathukamma as a state festival and distributes sarees to women across the state.

🪔
October / November

Diwali / Deepawali (దీపావళి)

Diwali — the Festival of Lights — is celebrated across India, and Laxmapuram embraces it with great enthusiasm. Homes are illuminated with rows of oil lamps (diyas) and colourful electric lights. Rangoli patterns adorn doorsteps and courtyards.

Children burst firecrackers and sparklers with glee, while adults perform Lakshmi Pooja in the evening, seeking the blessings of Goddess Lakshmi for wealth and prosperity. The occasion holds special significance in Laxmapuram given the village's devotion to Goddess Lakshmi. Sweets like Murukku, Laddu, and Halwa are prepared at home and shared with neighbours. The village temple conducts a special Deepa Utsavam where hundreds of lamps illuminate the deity.

Cultural Heritage Note

The festivals of Laxmapuram reflect the syncretic culture of Telangana — honouring nature cycles, agricultural seasons, village deities, and pan-Indian traditions. Each celebration reinforces community solidarity, intergenerational bonding, and the transmission of oral folklore, songs, and rituals to younger generations. Preserving these traditions is an act of cultural stewardship that the entire community participates in.

History of Laxmapuram

From ancient dynasties to a proud Telangana village — centuries of heritage

  1. Home
  2. History

The Name: Laxmapuram

A village blessed by the goddess of prosperity

The name Laxmapuram (లక్ష్మాపురం) is derived from Laxmi (Goddess Lakshmi, the Hindu deity of wealth, prosperity, and fortune) and puram (meaning "town" or "settlement" in Telugu/Sanskrit). The village is consecrated to the combined deity Sri Laxminarasimha Swamy — an embodiment of Goddess Lakshmi and Lord Narasimha (the fourth avatar of Vishnu), who is worshipped as the presiding deity and protector of the community.

Historical Timeline

Key eras that shaped our village

🏰
12th – 14th Century

Kakatiya Dynasty Era

The region now encompassing Laxmapuram and its surroundings was part of the prosperous Kakatiya kingdom, which ruled Telangana from their capital at Warangal. The Kakatiyas were great builders and patrons of Telugu culture and literature. Under their reign, tank irrigation flourished across the Deccan plateau, and many small agricultural settlements like Laxmapuram were established near water bodies to support farming communities.

The Kakatiyas constructed thousands of tanks (cheruvus) across the region — a tradition that shaped the hydraulic landscape of villages like Laxmapuram for centuries. The Ramannapet area, lying in the fertile upland plains, would have supported paddy cultivation, with communities gathering around these life-sustaining water reservoirs.

The dynasty's patronage of Shaivite and Vaishnava traditions also laid the foundations for the temple-centric village culture that Laxmapuram embodies today, with its prominent Laxminarasimha Swamy and Pochamma Devi temples.

⚔️
14th – 17th Century

Bahmani & Qutb Shahi Period

Following the weakening of the Kakatiya kingdom after Ulugh Khan's invasions in the early 14th century, the Deccan came under the influence of various Sultanates. The Bahmani Sultanate and later the Qutb Shahi dynasty of Golconda (founded around 1518) brought the Telangana region into a new administrative era.

During this period, villages like Laxmapuram continued to thrive as agricultural settlements. The Qutb Shahis encouraged trade and provided relative stability across the Deccan. Revenue records from this era (the earliest administrative documents of the region) began cataloguing villages and their agricultural outputs. The area around Ramannapet was known for its agricultural productivity and cattle-rearing traditions.

👑
17th – 20th Century

Nizam Era – Hyderabad State

With the rise of the Asaf Jahi dynasty (the Nizams of Hyderabad) in the 18th century, Laxmapuram became part of the vast Hyderabad State. The Nizams administered Telangana through a system of taluks and districts, and Ramannapet area fell under the Nalgonda district of Hyderabad State.

During the Nizam period, the village's traditional economy was largely agrarian. Farmers cultivated paddy, jowar, and pulses, using the village tank and seasonal rains. The Nizam's government maintained land revenue records (Pattadar Passbooks system), many of which still inform land ownership in the village today.

The early 20th century saw the rise of the Telangana peasant movement and the Nizam's resistance to integration with India. Despite political upheavals, village life in Laxmapuram continued its ancient rhythms — seasonal festivals, communal farming, and the care of village temples. The Pochamma Devi temple continued to be the spiritual anchor of the community through these times.

1947 – 1956

Indian Independence & Integration

India gained independence on August 15, 1947. However, the Nizam of Hyderabad initially resisted merger with the Indian Union. Following the Police Action (Operation Polo) of September 1948, Hyderabad State was integrated into the Indian Republic.

Laxmapuram and the surrounding villages of Ramannapet Mandal celebrated Indian Independence with great joy. The tri-colour flag was hoisted across village panchayats, and the era of democratic self-governance began. Local village panchayats were formally constituted, giving residents a voice in their own administration for the first time.

The States Reorganisation Act of 1956 created states along linguistic lines. Laxmapuram became part of the newly formed Andhra Pradesh state, which combined Telugu-speaking regions of Hyderabad State with the former Andhra State.

🌾
1956 – 2014

Andhra Pradesh State

As part of Andhra Pradesh, Laxmapuram benefited from decades of post-independence development. Green Revolution technologies reached the village in the 1960s–70s, introducing high-yield crop varieties, chemical fertilisers, and improved irrigation. The village tank was desilted and maintained, and bore wells were dug to supplement irrigation.

The construction of the Zilla Parishad (ZP) Primary School gave the village's children access to formal education. Healthcare improved with sub-centres established under national health programmes. The electrification of the village brought light, and gradually, paved roads connected Laxmapuram to Ramannapet and Hyderabad.

Through this era, the village panchayat system was strengthened, especially after the 73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992), which formally empowered gram panchayats. Laxmapuram's Gram Panchayat became an active unit of local self-government.

🌟
June 2, 2014 – Present

Telangana — A New State, A New Chapter

The long-cherished dream of a separate Telangana state was realised on June 2, 2014, when the state of Telangana was officially carved out of Andhra Pradesh. This was a moment of immense pride and celebration for the people of Laxmapuram and across the Telangana region.

Laxmapuram became part of the newly constituted Yadadri Bhuvanagiri District (formerly part of Nalgonda district), under Ramannapet Mandal. The Telangana government launched several flagship programmes that directly benefited the village: Mission Bhagiratha (piped drinking water), Palle Pragathi (village beautification), and extensive road improvement schemes.

Today, Laxmapuram is a proud village of Telangana — maintaining its rich agricultural heritage while embracing modern amenities, education, and connectivity. The village has been declared an Open Defecation Free (ODF) settlement under the Swachh Bharat Mission, reflecting the community's commitment to progress and dignity.

Agricultural Heritage

The land that feeds the community

🌾 Crops & Farming

Laxmapuram's fertile black cotton soil and red loam soil support a variety of crops. Paddy (rice) is the primary crop during the Kharif season, while Rabi cultivation includes jowar, bajra, and various pulses. Cotton, chilies, and groundnut are important commercial crops. Many households also maintain kitchen gardens and small orchards of mango and tamarind.

💧 Tank Irrigation (Cheruvu)

The village tank (cheruvu) is a centuries-old engineering marvel of the Kakatiya period. It collects monsoon runoff and channels water to agricultural fields through a traditional system of sluice gates and channels (kalvalu). In addition to the tank, bore wells drilled under government programmes provide supplemental irrigation. Mission Bhagiratha has also brought piped water to every household.

🐄 Livestock & Livelihoods

Cattle rearing has been an integral part of Laxmapuram's rural economy. Bullocks traditionally powered the plough, while cows and buffaloes provide milk for household use and sale. Goat rearing is another supplementary livelihood. Today, many families also participate in non-farm activities, with younger generations finding employment in Hyderabad and Ramannapet.

# History of Laxmapuram Village

**Village:** Laxmapuram (లక్ష్మాపురం)  
**Mandal:** Ramannapet  
**District:** Yadadri Bhuvanagiri  
**State:** Telangana, India  
**PIN:** 508113

---

## The Name: Laxmapuram

The name **Laxmapuram** derives from two words: *Laxmi* (Goddess Lakshmi, the Hindu deity of wealth and prosperity) and *puram* (meaning "settlement" or "town" in Telugu and Sanskrit). The village is consecrated to the combined deity **Sri Laxminarasimha Swamy** — an embodiment of Goddess Lakshmi and Lord Narasimha (the fourth avatar of Lord Vishnu). This deity is worshipped as the presiding divinity and protector of the village community, and the village's name reflects this sacred association.

The name also reflects the aspirations of its founders — a prosperous, goddess-blessed settlement where the community could flourish. Today, Laxmapuram continues to live up to its name as a self-sufficient agricultural community with strong cultural and spiritual roots.

---

## Kakatiya Dynasty Era (12th – 14th Century CE)

The region surrounding Laxmapuram and the broader Ramannapet area was part of the prosperous **Kakatiya kingdom**, which ruled from their capital at Warangal (modern Hanamkonda-Warangal urban agglomeration). The Kakatiyas were among the greatest rulers of Telangana and are celebrated for their military prowess, architectural achievements, and patronage of Telugu literature and art.

The Kakatiyas constructed thousands of tanks (*cheruvus*) and irrigation tanks across the Deccan plateau, transforming the agricultural landscape. Under their rule, many small farming settlements were established near water bodies — a pattern that Laxmapuram fits well. The village tank (cheruvu) that remains the irrigation lifeline of Laxmapuram today may trace its origins or early maintenance to the Kakatiya period.

The Kakatiyas were great patrons of both Shaivism and Vaishnavism, and their legacy in temple-building and village deity worship shaped the cultural DNA of villages across Telangana. The tradition of honouring the Grama Devata (village goddess — Pochamma/Yellamma) alongside Vaishnava deities like Narasimha is a direct inheritance from this era.

The Kakatiya dynasty weakened significantly after Ulugh Khan's invasions in the early 14th century, and the great empire fragmented. However, the village culture, tank-irrigation system, and temple traditions survived as enduring legacies across villages like Laxmapuram.

---

## Bahmani Sultanate and Qutb Shahi Period (14th – 17th Century)

Following the decline of the Kakatiya kingdom, the Deccan came under a succession of Sultanates. The **Bahmani Sultanate** (1347–1527 CE) and its successor, the **Qutb Shahi dynasty of Golconda** (c. 1518–1687 CE), administered the Telangana region for several centuries.

During the Qutb Shahi period, Golconda Fort and the surrounding diamond-trade city became famous, and the region experienced relative peace and prosperity. Villages like Laxmapuram continued their agricultural cycles largely undisturbed. The Qutb Shahis maintained revenue records and the *pattadar* (landholder) system that forms the basis of land ownership documentation even today.

The area around Ramannapet was noted for its agricultural productivity. Paddy cultivation, supported by tank irrigation, was the primary activity. The cultural and religious life of the village continued, with annual Jataras (village fairs) at the Pochamma Devi temple serving as important community events.

Telugu literature and culture flourished under the later Qutb Shahis, who patronised Telugu poets and maintained Telugu-speaking administrative officials. This period reinforced the Telugu language and cultural traditions that are deeply embedded in Laxmapuram's identity.

---

## Mughal and Nizam Era (17th – 20th Century)

When the Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb conquered Golconda in 1687, the entire Deccan came under Mughal suzerainty. However, effective control over distant villages like Laxmapuram remained largely through local chiefs and village headmen (*deshmukhs*).

The most significant period of Laxmapuram's pre-independence history came with the rise of the **Asaf Jahi dynasty** — the Nizams of Hyderabad. The **Nizam of Hyderabad** administered vast territories including all of Telangana from 1724 until 1948. Hyderabad State was one of the largest princely states in British India.

Under Nizam administration, Laxmapuram fell under the district of Nalgonda (which then included the Ramannapet area). The village economy was primarily agricultural, with paddy, jowar, bajra, and pulses as major crops. Farmers paid revenue to the state in cash or kind.

The early 20th century saw growing political consciousness in Hyderabad State. The **Telangana peasant movement** (1946–51), centred in the Nalgonda district, reflected the frustrations of farmers against feudal exploitation by large landholders (*deshmukhs* and *jagirdars*). Villages in the Ramannapet area were touched by this movement, and several farmers became aware of their rights for the first time.

Despite political upheavals, village life in Laxmapuram maintained its ancient rhythms — seasonal festivals, communal farming, and the care of village temples. The Pochamma Devi temple continued to be the spiritual anchor of the community throughout these centuries of change.

---

## Indian Independence and Integration (1947 – 1956)

India gained independence on **15 August 1947**. The Nizam of Hyderabad, however, initially sought either independence or accession to Pakistan rather than merger with India. Following diplomatic negotiations and the subsequent **Police Action (Operation Polo)** of September 17–18, 1948, Hyderabad State was integrated into the Indian Republic.

For the people of Laxmapuram, this was a moment of historic joy. The tri-colour Indian flag was hoisted at the village panchayat, and the era of democratic self-governance began. Local elections were held for the first time, and village residents gained the right to choose their own leaders.

The **States Reorganisation Act of 1956** redrew India's state boundaries along linguistic lines. Laxmapuram became part of the newly formed **Andhra Pradesh** state, which united the Telugu-speaking areas of Hyderabad State (Telangana region) with the former Andhra State (coastal and Rayalaseema Andhra regions). This merger was accepted, though it would later become a source of regional identity conflicts.

---

## Andhra Pradesh Period (1956 – 2014)

As part of Andhra Pradesh, Laxmapuram experienced decades of development alongside the broader transformation of rural India.

The **Green Revolution** of the 1960s and 70s reached the village with high-yield crop varieties, chemical fertilisers, and improved irrigation equipment. Agricultural productivity increased significantly. The village tank was desilted and maintained by the panchayat. Bore wells were sunk with government support, providing supplemental irrigation and drinking water.

The construction of the **Zilla Parishad (ZP) Primary School** in the village gave Laxmapuram's children access to free primary education. Health improvements followed, with government sub-centres providing basic maternal and child health services. Electrification transformed daily life — from kerosene lamps to electric light — and gradually enabled the use of electric water pumps, fans, and eventually televisions.

The **73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992)** formally empowered Gram Panchayats as institutions of local self-government. Laxmapuram's Gram Panchayat became an active unit, with elected ward members and a Sarpanch responsible for local governance. Women's reservation in panchayat elections brought new voices into village leadership.

Throughout the Andhra Pradesh period, Telangana's regional identity gradually asserted itself. Grievances over unequal development, water sharing, and government employment created a growing demand for a separate Telangana state — a movement that would achieve its goal in 2014.

---

## Telangana — A New State, A New Chapter (2014 – Present)

The dream of a separate **Telangana state** was realised on **June 2, 2014**, when the state was officially carved out of Andhra Pradesh by an Act of Parliament. This was a historic moment of pride and celebration for the people of Laxmapuram and across the region. The Telangana state's formation was the culmination of over six decades of political struggle.

Laxmapuram became part of the newly constituted **Yadadri Bhuvanagiri District** (reorganised from the former Nalgonda district) under **Ramannapet Mandal**. The village's constituency was confirmed as the **Ramannapet Assembly Constituency**, under the **Bhongir Lok Sabha Constituency**.

The Telangana government launched several flagship programmes that directly benefited Laxmapuram:

- **Mission Bhagiratha** — piped drinking water supplied to every household
- **Palle Pragathi** — village beautification, road improvement, and plantation drives
- **Rythu Bandhu** — agricultural investment support scheme for farmers
- **KCR Kit / Aarogyasri** — healthcare support programmes
- **Swachh Bharat Mission** — Laxmapuram achieved ODF (Open Defecation Free) status

Today, Laxmapuram stands as a proud Telangana village — maintaining its rich agricultural heritage, vibrant festival culture, and deep spiritual traditions, while embracing the opportunities of the modern era. The village's young people travel to Hyderabad and beyond for education and employment, but the bonds to the home village remain strong.

## Agricultural Heritage

Agriculture is the foundation of Laxmapuram's economy and identity. The village's fertile land supports **paddy (rice)** as the primary Kharif crop, with **jowar, bajra, pulses (kandi, pesalu), cotton, and chillies** grown as Rabi and commercial crops. Many households maintain **mango, tamarind, and neem** trees as part of their domestic landscape.

The **village tank (cheruvu)** is a centuries-old engineering feat that collects monsoon runoff and channels water to agricultural fields. The traditional system of sluice gates and earthen channels (*kalvalu*) has been maintained by farmers across generations. In addition, **bore wells** drilled with government support provide supplemental irrigation.

**Cattle and livestock** remain important to village livelihoods. Bullocks once powered the plough, and many families continue to rear cattle, goats, and poultry. The dairy tradition — with fresh milk supplied to local households and markets — represents a significant secondary income source.

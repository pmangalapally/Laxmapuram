# Festivals and Cultural Traditions of Laxmapuram

**Village:** Laxmapuram (లక్ష్మాపురం)  
**Mandal:** Ramannapet, Yadadri Bhuvanagiri District, Telangana  

---

## Overview

Festivals are the living heartbeat of Laxmapuram. They mark the agricultural calendar, honour divine beings, celebrate seasonal transitions, and strengthen the bonds of community. Every festival in the village is a collective experience — families gather, neighbours share food, the young learn from the old, and the sacred mingles with the joyful.

Laxmapuram's festival calendar reflects the beautiful confluence of pan-Indian Hindu traditions and the distinctly Telangana cultural identity — particularly visible in celebrations like Bathukamma, Bonalu, and the Village Jatara.

---

## Makar Sankranti (సంక్రాంతి) — January

**When:** January 14–16 (three days)  
**Significance:** Harvest festival; the sun's northward journey (Uttarayana); thanksgiving to nature

Sankranti is arguably the most widely celebrated festival in Laxmapuram and across rural Telangana. It marks the end of the harvest season and expresses gratitude to the sun, nature, and the cattle that till the land.

### Bhogi (Day 1 — January 13)
The festival begins with **Bhogi** — the evening before Sankranti. Families light a bonfire (*bhogi mantalu*) at the entrance of their home and burn old, unwanted items — symbolising the destruction of the past and the welcoming of new beginnings. Children traditionally throw a mix of *regi pandu* (ber/jujube fruits), sugarcane pieces, and flowers over one another's heads as a blessing ritual.

### Sankranti (Day 2 — January 14)
This is the main festival day. Families rise early, take an oil bath, and wear new clothes. Women draw intricate rangoli (*muggu*) patterns at the doorsteps — often featuring geometric designs, peacocks, and lotuses. Fresh rice is cooked as sweet Pongal (with jaggery, milk, and sesame) and offered to the Sun God.

**Haridasus** — wandering minstrels in elaborate costumes representing various divine characters — visit homes throughout the Sankranti season, singing devotional songs in praise of Lord Vishnu. They receive alms of rice, grains, and money. Their arrival is eagerly anticipated by children.

The skies fill with colourful kites as children and adults compete in kite-flying contests. The village buzzes with the sounds of kites swooping and the shouts of competitors trying to cut each other's strings.

Traditional Sankranti sweets prepared in Laxmapuram include:
- **Ariselu** (deep-fried rice and jaggery cakes)
- **Poli / Bobbattu** (stuffed sweet flatbread with lentil or coconut filling)
- **Sesame Laddu** (til ke ladoo)
- **Jaggery-based Payasam** (sweet milk pudding)

### Mattu Pongal (Day 3 — January 15)
Cattle are bathed, decorated with paint, garlands of marigold, and bells, and paraded through the village as a mark of gratitude for their service in agriculture. This tradition honours the animals that sustain the farming community.

## Maha Shivaratri (శివరాత్రి) — February/March

**When:** 14th night of the dark fortnight in Phalguna month (February–March)  
**Significance:** The great night of Lord Shiva; cosmic wedding of Shiva and Parvati

Maha Shivaratri is observed with deep devotion at the **Shiva Temple** in Laxmapuram. Devotees observe a strict fast throughout the day, and many maintain a night-long vigil (*jaagaran*), chanting *"Om Namah Shivaya"*.

Offerings of bilva (bel) leaves, milk, curd, honey, water, and sacred ash (*vibhuti*) are made to the Shivalingam. The temple is illuminated with oil lamps, and the air is heavy with incense smoke. Priests conduct special abhishekam ceremonies at midnight — the most auspicious time of Shivaratri.

Cultural programmes — including retellings of Shiva's stories, classical music, and devotional songs — are held through the night. The mythological significance of the night (Shiva's Tandava dance, the wedding of Shiva and Parvati, Shiva saving the universe by drinking poison) is narrated to devotees.

## Ugadi (ఉగాది) — March/April

**When:** First day of Chaitra month (Telugu/Kannada New Year)  
**Significance:** Telugu New Year; beginning of a new Samvatsara (year in the Hindu lunar calendar)

Ugadi is one of the most important festivals of the Telugu calendar. In Laxmapuram, preparations begin days before — homes are cleaned and whitewashed, mango leaf *toranam* (festoon) is hung at doorways, and new clothes are purchased.

### Ugadi Pachadi
The defining ritual of Ugadi is the preparation and tasting of **Ugadi Pachadi** — a mixture of six ingredients representing the six emotions of life:

| Ingredient | Taste | Emotion/Life Experience |
|------------|-------|------------------------|
| Raw mango | Tangy/Surprise | The unexpected |
| Jaggery | Sweet | Happiness and joy |
| Neem flowers | Bitter | Sorrow and hardship |
| Tamarind | Sour | Disgust and unpleasantness |
| Green chili | Spicy/Pungent | Anger |
| Salt | Salty | Fear |

This symbolic blend reminds devotees that life is a complete experience — containing all these flavours — and must be accepted with equanimity.

### Panchanga Sravanam
The village priest reads aloud the *Panchanga* (almanac) — the astrological predictions for the coming year, including rainfall forecasts, crop prospects, and significant dates. Villagers gather to hear these predictions with keen interest.

Families visit elders and teachers to receive blessings. Temples conduct special poojas and Ugadi food — including **Pulihora** (tamarind rice), **Bobbatlu** (sweet flatbread), and **Mango rice** — is distributed as prasadam.

## Sri Ram Navami (రామ నవమి) — March/April

**When:** 9th day (Navami) of Chaitra Shukla Paksha (bright fortnight)  
**Significance:** Birthday of Lord Rama; celebration of righteousness (dharma)

Ram Navami is celebrated with great devotion at the **Sri Rama Temple** in Laxmapuram. Devotees observe fasting and gather at the temple for special abhishekam of the deity.

The highlight is the **Sita Kalyana** ceremony — the symbolic and joyful re-enactment of the wedding of Lord Rama and Goddess Sita. This is performed amidst the chanting of Vedic hymns and the distribution of prasadam.

Traditional Ram Navami prasadam includes:
- **Panakam** — jaggery water flavoured with cardamom, dried ginger, and pepper
- **Vadapappu** — soaked and seasoned raw green gram
- **Fruits** — offered to the deity and distributed

Recitations and readings from the Ramayana (Valmiki Ramayana or the Telugu Ranganatha Ramayana) are conducted at the temple. Some years, local artists perform a stage adaptation of scenes from the Ramayana.

## Bonalu (బోనాలు) — July/August

**When:** During the months of Ashadha and Shravana (July–August)  
**Significance:** Telangana's iconic festival honouring the Mother Goddess (Mahakali, Pochamma)

Bonalu is among the most distinctly Telangana festivals and holds special pride in Laxmapuram. The word "Bonalu" derives from the Telugu word for "Bhojanam" (food offering). It is a festival in which women offer cooked rice in earthen pots to the mother goddess as a mark of gratitude and devotion.

### Preparations
Families begin preparations days in advance. Women fast and observe rituals. New earthen pots are purchased and decorated. Rice is cooked with turmeric, jaggery, and sometimes curd — the *bonam* (offering).

### The Procession
On the main day, women dressed in colourful new sarees and adorned with flowers carry the decorated pots (*bonalu*) balanced on their heads to the **Pochamma Devi Temple**. The procession is accompanied by the rhythmic and energetic beats of **dhol-tasha** drums, creating an electrifying atmosphere. Men line the streets to watch and offer support.

The sight of hundreds of women walking in colourful procession, carrying earthen pots on their heads, with drums reverberating through the village, is one of the most spectacular expressions of Telangana culture.

### Rangam
A distinctive feature of Bonalu is **Rangam** — a form of prophecy performed by the temple priest or a designated woman who enters a trance-like state, believed to be possessed by the goddess's spirit. She utters predictions and blessings for the community for the coming year.

Bonalu was declared an official state festival by the Telangana government after 2014, and its recognition has deepened the community's pride in this celebration.

## Village Jatara (జాతర) — Annual

**When:** Annually, usually in summer or as per the temple calendar  
**Significance:** Annual village fair at the Pochamma/Yellamma Devi temple; community reunion

The **Village Jatara** is the grandest and most eagerly anticipated event of the year in Laxmapuram. It is an annual fair held at the **Pochamma Devi / Yellamma Devi Temple** — the Grama Devata (village goddess) shrine.

### Duration and Activities
The Jatara typically lasts **2–3 days** and draws visitors and former residents from surrounding villages and towns. The event includes:

- **Special temple poojas** and ritual offerings
- **Animal offerings** (as per traditional practice)
- **Folk performances** — Burrakatha (ballad storytelling), Kolata (stick dance), Veeranatyam (devotional acrobatics)
- **Village fair** with stalls selling toys, sweets (Jangiri, Gulab Jamun, Murmura), bangles, household utensils, and agricultural tools
- **Cultural programmes** in the evenings — music, dance, drama

### Social Significance
The Jatara is as much a social event as a religious one. Families that have moved to cities return to the village for the Jatara, renewing connections with relatives and friends. Long-standing friendships are rekindled over shared meals. The entire event is managed by the village community and the temple committee through voluntary contributions.

## Bathukamma (బతుకమ్మ) — September/October

**When:** Nine days during Navaratri season (Bhadrapada/Ashwin month)  
**Significance:** Telangana's unique floral festival; celebration of womanhood and nature; prayer to Goddess Gauri/Parvati

Bathukamma is the most uniquely Telangana festival — a celebration that has no parallel elsewhere in India. The name translates as "Mother, come alive" (*batuku* = life, *amma* = mother) — a prayer to the goddess to bless all women with long, healthy, and prosperous lives.

### Making the Bathukamma
Women and girls of Laxmapuram gather seasonal wildflowers in the days leading up to the festival. The characteristic flowers of Bathukamma include:
- **Tangidi** (*Cassia auriculata* / Tanner's cassia)
- **Gunugu** (*Celosia argentea* / Cock's comb)
- **Banti** (Marigold)
- **Teku** (*Tecoma stans* / Yellow bells)
- **Kaasi** and other local seasonal blooms

These flowers are stacked in concentric layers over a broad base, building upward into a conical, tiered tower that can reach impressive heights. The arrangement is placed in a brass plate or shallow basket.

### The Evening Ritual (Nine Days)
Each evening for nine days, women dress in new or traditional clothes and carry their Bathukammas to the village ground, open space near the temple, or the village tank. They set the Bathukammas down in a circle, join hands, and dance around them in a circle (*Eppa adutaru*), singing traditional Bathukamma songs.

The songs — passed down orally across generations — invoke Goddess Gauri, narrate mythological stories, describe nature, celebrate sisterhood, and express the joys and sorrows of womanhood. The rhythmic handclapping and melodic singing create an atmosphere of communal joy.

### Saddula Bathukamma (Final Day)
The ninth and final day — **Saddula Bathukamma** — is the most elaborate. Women prepare the largest and most beautifully adorned Bathukammas. The final procession moves to the village tank or pond, where the Bathukammas are immersed in the water as an offering to the goddess, allowing the flowers to return to nature.

The Telangana government now observes Bathukamma as an official state festival, distributing sarees to women across the state on this occasion.

## Diwali / Deepawali (దీపావళి) — October/November

**When:** Amavasya (new moon night) of Kartika month (October–November)  
**Significance:** Festival of Lights; victory of light over darkness; worship of Goddess Lakshmi

Diwali holds special significance in Laxmapuram because of the village's devotion to **Goddess Lakshmi** (from whom the village takes its name). The festival celebrates the return of Lord Rama to Ayodhya, the victory of Lord Krishna over the demon Narakasura (Naraka Chaturdashi), and the worship of Goddess Lakshmi for wealth and prosperity.

### Preparations
Homes are cleaned, whitewashed, and decorated with strings of lights. Rangoli patterns — often featuring footprints of Goddess Lakshmi leading into the home — adorn courtyards and doorsteps. New clothes are purchased.

### Naraka Chaturdashi (Day 1)
The day before Diwali (Naraka Chaturdashi) commemorates Lord Krishna's defeat of the demon Narakasura. Families wake before dawn, apply sesame oil bath, and burst firecrackers at sunrise to symbolise the demon's destruction.

### Lakshmi Pooja (Diwali Night)
The main Diwali evening features the **Lakshmi Pooja** — a ritual worship of Goddess Lakshmi performed with incense, flowers, sweets, and lamps. Homes are lit with rows of earthen diyas (oil lamps). The Laxminarasimha Swamy temple conducts a special **Deepa Utsavam** (festival of lamps), where hundreds of oil lamps illuminate the deity and the temple premises.

Children burst firecrackers and sparklers with great enthusiasm. Traditional Diwali sweets — **Murukku**, **Chakli**, **Besan Laddu**, **Gulab Jamun**, and **Halwa** — are prepared at home and distributed to neighbours.

## Cultural Notes on Laxmapuram's Festival Traditions

### Communal Harmony
The festivals of Laxmapuram bring together all sections of the community — across caste, economic status, and age. The shared preparation, cooking, processions, and worship create bonds that transcend everyday social divisions. Neighbours share festival food (*prasadam*) and participate in each other's celebrations.

### Women's Centrality
Festivals like Bathukamma, Bonalu, and Ugadi place women at the centre — as singers, cooks, ritual leaders, and primary participants. The oral traditions of Telangana — folk songs, lullabies, and festival chants — are preserved and transmitted primarily by women across generations.

### Agricultural Calendar Connection
Most festivals are timed to agricultural seasons: Sankranti (post-harvest), Ugadi (sowing season/New Year), Bathukamma (post-monsoon, flower season), and Bonalu (monsoon season). This calendar alignment reinforces the sacred relationship between farming, nature, and community thanksgiving.

### Preservation Efforts
As younger generations move to cities, there is a conscious effort within Laxmapuram to maintain these traditions. Teaching festival songs to children, preserving Bathukamma flower-stacking techniques, and continuing the Jatara rituals are acts of cultural stewardship that the community takes seriously.
